Journal Entry

October 27th, 1923 – Chicago, Illinois

Something arrived tonight. It didn’t come by mail.

It was leaning against the door of my office when I returned from dinner — a crumpled brown envelope, no postmark, no seal, no name. Not a single word written. Just a faint scent of smoke and a dampness, as if it had passed through a place not bound by time.

Inside were four photographs. Old, grainy, blurred. But recent enough to unsettle me.

The first shows a stone corridor, dimly lit by a single lantern. The walls are covered in moss… and markings. The same markings. Three vertical lines inside a rough circle.

The second… is an empty room. But something is wrong with the light. It looks… warped. As if gravity itself had stopped working properly.

The third is the hardest to look at. A hooded figure standing among dead trees. The face is a smear of shadow, and where eyes should be, there’s only a dull reflection — like the gleam of a buried mirror.

The last photo shows a hand. Bloodied. Holding a medallion I instantly recognize: it belongs to Langley.

No explanation. No signature. But on the back of the fourth photograph, written in small, trembling letters:

“He crossed over. Now, you.”

I think I won’t sleep tonight. And I believe that was the point.

But what haunts me more is the question I keep circling: Who sent this?

Someone wants to rattle me. That much is clear. Someone who knows about Langley. Someone who has access to these… places. Images.

And tonight, the silence is screaming.
